Monkey grass, a common name for plants like Liriope (Liriope muscari) and Mondo Grass (Ophiopogon japonicus), is a popular choice for borders, ground cover, and accent planting due to its lush, evergreen foliage and low-maintenance nature. While these resilient plants require minimal care, proper trimming at the right time is key to keeping them looking their best, promoting healthy new growth, and preventing them from becoming unruly. This guide will walk you through exactly how and when to trim your monkey grass border for optimal health and aesthetics.

---

## What is Monkey Grass?

Before diving into trimming, it's helpful to understand the two main types often referred to as "monkey grass":

* **Liriope (Liriope muscari):** Also known as Lilyturf, this is the most common variety. It has wider, strappy leaves, often forms clumps, and produces spikes of purple or white flowers in late summer, followed by black berries. Varieties like 'Big Blue' or 'Royal Purple' are popular.
* **Mondo Grass (Ophiopogon japonicus):** This typically has finer, darker green, almost grass-like leaves and grows more slowly than liriope. It also produces small white or lavender flowers, often hidden among the foliage, followed by blue-black berries. Dwarf varieties are also available.

Both are incredibly versatile and relatively drought-tolerant once established, but their evergreen nature means old foliage can accumulate and look untidy over time.

---

## Why Trim Monkey Grass?

Trimming serves several important purposes for monkey grass borders:

1.  **Aesthetics:** Removes old, discolored, or damaged foliage, giving the border a fresh, clean, and uniform appearance.
2.  **Promotes New Growth:** Cutting back old leaves encourages the plant to put energy into producing vibrant new green shoots, leading to a denser, healthier border.
3.  **Prevents Disease:** Removing old, matted foliage improves air circulation around the plant crowns, which can help prevent fungal diseases that thrive in damp, stagnant conditions.
4.  **Controls Spread (for some varieties):** While not its primary purpose, trimming can help manage the visual spread and neatness of clumping varieties.

## When to Trim Monkey Grass

Timing is critical for successful monkey grass trimming to avoid damaging new growth and to protect the plant from harsh weather conditions.

### The Best Time: Late Winter to Early Spring

The ideal window for trimming monkey grass is in **late winter to early spring**, typically from **February to March** in most temperate climates, or just before new growth begins to emerge.

**Why this time is best:**
* **Dormancy:** The plants are still relatively dormant, so you won't be cutting off active new shoots.
* **Protection:** The old foliage has offered some winter protection against cold and frost.
* **Easy Cleanup:** The old leaves are often dry and easier to cut and rake away.
* **Quick Rebound:** The warming temperatures and impending growing season mean the plants will quickly send up new, lush green blades shortly after trimming, covering up the cut stubs.

### When to Avoid Trimming:

* **Late Spring/Summer:** Avoid trimming once new growth has actively started or during the growing season. You'll be cutting off the very leaves the plant needs for photosynthesis and growth, weakening it.
* **Fall/Early Winter:** While tempting to tidy up before winter, trimming too late in the year (after early fall) can expose the plant's crown to freezing temperatures and frost damage, as the old foliage provides a layer of insulation.

## How to Trim Monkey Grass

There are two primary methods for trimming monkey grass, depending on the size of your border and your preference: manual shearing or mowing.

### Method 1: Manual Shearing (Best for Small to Medium Borders)

This method provides precise control and is ideal for intricate borders or smaller patches.

**Tools Needed:**
* Sharp hand shears
* Hedge shears (manual or electric)
* Gloves and safety glasses (always recommended)
* Rake or tarp for cleanup

**Steps:**

1.  **Prepare Your Tools:** Ensure your shears are clean and very sharp. Dull blades can tear the foliage, leading to ragged cuts and potential disease entry points.
2.  **Clear Debris:** Remove any leaves, sticks, or other debris that may have accumulated in the monkey grass.
3.  **Cut Back Foliage:** Using your hand or hedge shears, cut the monkey grass back to approximately **2 to 4 inches from the ground**. Aim for a uniform height across the border. You can collect handfuls of grass and shear them, or just sweep the shears across the top.
4.  **Clean Up:** Rake up all the cut foliage. Leftover clippings can smother new growth or harbor pests/diseases.
5.  **Optional: Fertilize and Redefine:** Once cleared, you can apply a balanced, slow-release granular fertilizer formulated for ornamental grasses or shrubs. You can also take this opportunity to redefine the edge of your border with a sharp spade or edger for a crisp look.

### Method 2: Mowing (Best for Large Borders or Ground Cover)

For extensive areas of monkey grass, a lawnmower can be a time-saving solution.

**Tools Needed:**
* Lawnmower (rotary mower works well)
* Gloves and safety glasses
* Rake or leaf blower for cleanup

**Steps:**

1.  **Set Mower Height:** Crucially, set your lawnmower to its **highest possible cutting height**. You want to cut the monkey grass back, but not scalp it. Ideally, you still want to leave 2-4 inches of stubble.
2.  **Ensure Dryness:** It's best to mow when the monkey grass is dry. Wet foliage can clump and clog the mower.
3.  **Clear Debris:** Before mowing, clear the area of any large sticks, rocks, or other objects that could be thrown by the mower or damage it.
4.  **Mow the Area:** Carefully mow over the monkey grass. Go slowly to ensure an even cut. You might need to make a couple of passes to get all the old foliage.
5.  **Clean Up:** Immediately rake or blow away all the clippings.
6.  **Optional: Fertilize and Redefine:** As with manual shearing, consider fertilizing lightly and cleaning up the border edges.

## Tips for Success

* **Sharp Tools are Non-Negotiable:** This is the most important rule. Sharp blades make clean cuts that heal faster and look better.
* **Don't Cut Too Low:** Never cut monkey grass flush with the ground. Leaving a few inches of old growth helps protect the crown and allows new shoots to emerge more easily.
* **Consider Division:** Early spring, when you're trimming, is also an excellent time to divide overgrown clumps of monkey grass. Dig up the clump, separate it into smaller sections, and replant or share.
* **Water After Trimming:** If the soil is dry after trimming, a good watering can help the plants recover and encourage new growth.
* **Brown Tips Throughout the Year:** If you notice brown tips on your monkey grass during the growing season, it's often a sign of stress (e.g., drought, too much sun, or over-fertilization). You can snip off individual brown tips with scissors for aesthetic purposes, but avoid a full trim until the next late winter/early spring.

By following these guidelines, your monkey grass borders will remain neat, healthy, and a beautiful feature of your landscape year after year.
